## Required Sections

- MUST include a "Hero / Positioning Statement" section: The opening section of your portfolio that communicates who you are, what level you operate at, and the type of problems you solve — all within seconds.
- MUST include a "Curated Case Studies (3–4 projects)" section: The core of your portfolio: a carefully selected set of case studies that together demonstrate the breadth and depth appropriate to your level.
- MUST include a "Impact & Metrics Summary" section: A visible layer — either on the homepage, within case study thumbnails, or in a dedicated section — that surfaces the business outcomes of your work at a glance.
- MUST include a "Design Process & Decision-Making Narrative" section: The section within each case study (or as a standalone philosophy page) that reveals how you think, make trade-offs, and navigate ambiguity.
- MUST include a "Leadership, Collaboration & Stakeholder Influence" section: Evidence of how you work with others, lead without authority, and influence outcomes beyond your individual design work.
- MUST include a "Systems Thinking & Craft Excellence" section: Evidence of your ability to think in systems — design systems, information architecture, component libraries, or product ecosystems — alongside demonstrations of visual and interaction craft.
- MUST include a "About / Design Philosophy & Point of View" section: A section that communicates your design philosophy, values, and unique perspective — helping hiring managers understand not just what you do, but how you think.

## Optional Sections

- SHOULD include a "AI Fluency & Emerging Practice" section: Evidence that you're adapting to the evolving design landscape, particularly around AI tools, AI-native design patterns, and new ways of working.

## Rules for Senior Level

- Your hero section must immediately convey your seniority signal and domain focus.
- **Senior:** Lead with a clear statement of your craft specialty (e.g., 'Senior Product Designer focused on complex B2B workflows') and a concise value proposition that connects your design work to business outcomes.
- **Senior:** Include at least one deep case study showing end-to-end craft (research → design → ship → measure), one that shows collaboration and speed, and optionally one small experiment showing curiosity .
- Senior designers speak in business metrics because that's the language executives understand .
- **Senior:** Surface key metrics directly in your case study thumbnails or summaries.
- **Senior:** Show your design process clearly and concisely .
- At senior-and-above levels, design is fundamentally collaborative.
- **Senior:** Clearly describe your role versus the team's contributions.
- 90% of hiring managers consider portfolios important when evaluating UX candidates , and at senior+ levels, evidence of leadership is often the deciding factor between a 'maybe' and a 'yes.'
- **Senior:** Show that you can build within and contribute to design systems.
- **Senior:** Your about section should convey your design philosophy, areas of specialization, and what kind of problems energize you.
- **Senior:** Show that you can design for AI-powered features (e.g., recommendation engines, smart defaults, LLM interfaces) or that you use AI tools effectively in your design process.

- **Principal:** Your process narrative should emphasize problem framing and scoping.

## Rules for Staff Level

- **Staff:** Position yourself as a design leader who drives influence across the organization.
- Show that you operate at the 'what should we build and why' level, not just 'how should we build it.' 

**Staff:** Your case studies should demonstrate org-wide or multi-product influence.
- **Staff:** Your metrics should reflect organizational scale: team velocity improvements, design system adoption rates across product lines, reduction in design debt, influence on company-level OKRs.
- **Staff:** Your process section should demonstrate meta-design thinking — how you establish processes and frameworks that other designers follow.
- **Staff:** Show organizational leadership.
- **Staff:** Show how you've driven design system strategy across an organization, established quality standards that scale, or influenced the technical architecture of products through design.
- **Staff:** Your about section should read almost like a design leadership manifesto.
- **Staff:** Show how you're shaping your organization's approach to AI in design — establishing guidelines, influencing AI product strategy, or defining how the design team should adopt AI tools responsibly.

## Case Study Rules

- MUST include at least 6 artifacts per case study
- Recommended length: Senior: 1,200–2,000 words per case study with 8–15 supporting visuals. Principal: 1,500–2,500 words emphasizing strategic context and cross-functional dynamics. Staff: 1,500–2,500 words with emphasis on organizational narrative, potentially including diagrams of team structures, influence maps, or design system architectures. † All levels should be scannable — use clear headers, pull quotes for key metrics, and progressive disclosure where possible [1][3].

### Context & Problem Framing
- Start with a clear 'before' picture — what was the business or user problem you were tasked to solve . This shows you think strategically and connect your work to business objectives from the start . Focus on the user problem to be solved and tell us how you knew there was a problem using feedback, testing, or data rather than explanations like 'the product manager said so' .

**Senior:** Frame the problem in terms of both user pain and business impact. Show that you understood the 'why' behind the project, not just the 'what.' 

**Principal:** Show that you participated in framing the problem itself — that you didn't just receive a brief, you shaped it. Include evidence of stakeholder alignment, strategic context, or opportunity identification. 

**Staff:** Frame the problem in terms of organizational or portfolio-level strategy. How did this project fit into the company's broader vision? Why was this the right problem to solve now?

### Your Role & Scope of Influence
- Hiring managers want project overviews summarizing the team makeup and the candidate's role on the team . Be honest about your scope — inflating your role is easily spotted in interviews. 

**Senior:** State your direct responsibilities (e.g., 'I led the UX design and conducted all user research for this feature area'). Name collaborators and describe the team structure. 

**Principal:** Describe your strategic responsibilities: 'I defined the design vision for this product area and coordinated design execution across three squads.' Show that your scope extended beyond individual feature delivery. 

**Staff:** Describe your organizational role: 'I sponsored this initiative, aligned leadership on the approach, and mentored the design team that executed the work.' Show that your value was in direction-setting and enabling others.

### Research, Discovery & Insight Generation
- UX and product design are all about people. The strongest portfolios make it clear that your choices were rooted in user insights . Rhonda Gilligan recommends: 'Tell us how you knew there was a problem using feedback, testing, or data' .

**Senior:** Show the research methods you used and the key insights that emerged. Include artifacts like journey maps, interview synthesis, or data analysis. Dan Sullivan's portfolio references specific numbers (e.g., '7 user interviews,' '70 unmoderated usability tests') . 

**Principal:** Emphasize how you directed the research strategy, not just conducted it. Show how you synthesized insights across multiple sources to form a strategic point of view. 

**Staff:** Show how you established research practices or influenced the organization's approach to evidence-based design. Your research section may be less about doing the research and more about how you ensured the right research happened.

### Design Exploration & Decision-Making
- A concise story of how and why you did what you did, not just a list of deliverables . Show examples of artifacts and deliverables like sketches, wireframes, mocks, and prototypes . But critically, explain the testing you did to validate your design and show how you iterated based on the results . Hiring managers want to see iterative design based on feedback .

**Senior:** Show 2–3 directions you explored and explain why you chose the one you did. Include evidence of iteration — how feedback changed your design. Show that you can move from low to high fidelity with intentionality. 

**Principal:** Emphasize the strategic trade-offs. Why did you prioritize one approach over another? How did constraints (technical, business, timeline) shape the solution? Show that your decision-making considered multiple stakeholder perspectives. 

**Staff:** Your exploration section may focus more on how you set up the decision framework — how you empowered designers to explore and make good decisions. Show the organizational mechanisms you created for design critique and quality assurance.

### Final Solution & Design Rationale
- This is where your craft shines — but it must be connected to the problem you framed at the beginning. Screens should be shown with just enough context to understand the decisions behind them . Don't just show mockups — annotate them, explain micro-decisions, and connect visual choices back to user needs and business goals. 

**Senior:** Show polished, production-ready designs with annotations explaining key decisions. Include interaction details, responsive considerations, and accessibility choices where relevant. 

**Principal:** Frame the solution in terms of the system you designed, not just the screens. Show how components, patterns, and flows work together as a coherent product experience. 

**Staff:** Your solution presentation should demonstrate the design vision you set that others executed against. Show how the solution reflects the principles and standards you established.

### Outcomes, Metrics & Business Impact
- Hard numbers — metrics, KPIs, and analytics that prove your solution worked — this is the ultimate proof of value . Revenue growth, retention spikes, efficiency gains with hard numbers are what separate senior portfolios . If you don't have exact revenue numbers, focus on measurable proxies: support ticket decrease, task completion time, adoption rates .

**Senior:** Include 2–3 specific metrics tied to your design work. Dan Sullivan shows metrics like '77% adoption,' '44% increase in clicks,' and '352% increase in survey participation' . Be honest about what you can and can't attribute directly to design. 

**Principal:** Frame outcomes in terms of strategic impact — not just 'conversion went up 15%' but 'this validated our bet on self-service and informed the roadmap for the next two quarters.'  Show how outcomes influenced future decisions. 

**Staff:** Show organizational outcomes alongside product outcomes — did this project establish a new practice? Did it change how the company makes design decisions? Did it elevate the design team's influence?

### Reflection, Learnings & What's Next
- This section is optional but highly differentiated at senior+ levels. It signals maturity, self-awareness, and a growth mindset — all qualities hiring managers prize. 

**Senior:** Reflect on what you learned about your craft, your process, or working with the team. What would you do differently? What surprised you? 

**Principal:** Reflect on strategic learnings — what did this project teach you about product strategy, organizational dynamics, or design leadership? 

**Staff:** Reflect on what this project revealed about the design organization's maturity, processes, or culture — and how you acted on those insights.

## MUST NOT

- MUST NOT: The 'junior in disguise' portfolio — showing only execution and deliverables without strategic context, regardless of years of experience [1]
- MUST NOT: The 'everything portfolio' — including every project, every step, every screen in an attempt to be thorough, resulting in a long, repetitive experience [2]
- MUST NOT: Process theater — showing a generic double-diamond or design thinking framework without connecting it to actual decisions made in the project [2] †
- MUST NOT: Metrics afterthought — burying business outcomes at the bottom of a case study instead of leading with impact [1]
- MUST NOT: Solo hero narrative — taking credit for everything without acknowledging team, constraints, or organizational context [6] [8]
- MUST NOT: Beauty-first, thinking-second — a portfolio that's visually stunning but reveals no evidence of strategic thinking, user research, or design rationale [6]
- MUST NOT: One-size-fits-all portfolio — not tailoring your portfolio to the specific type of role or company you're targeting. 'If your portfolio could work for any company, it works for no company' [2]
- MUST NOT: Ignoring the portfolio's own UX — poor navigation, slow loading, broken links, non-responsive layouts, or walls of text without visual hierarchy [8]
- MUST NOT: Concept-only portfolios at senior+ levels — while small experiments have their place [2], a senior portfolio should primarily feature shipped work that survived real-world constraints [6]
- MUST NOT: AI-washing — adding AI buzzwords without genuine understanding or meaningful application of AI in design practice †

## What Hiring Managers Evaluate

- Evidence of business impact with real metrics — hiring managers between 3–10 seconds are skimming for metrics, outcomes, and strategic thinking . This is the single fastest way to move from the 'maybe' pile to the 'yes' pile.
- Clear storytelling with a problem-solution-outcome arc — 'Tell a story. People like to be entertained and hiring managers are no different. What was the conflict? What was the resolution?'
- Honest scoping of your role vs. team contributions — inflated claims are immediately obvious in interviews and erode trust
- Demonstration of iterative, feedback-driven design — 'I'm always looking for a project or two that shows iterative design based on feedback'
- Appropriate altitude for seniority level — a senior should show craft depth and ownership, a principal should show strategic influence and cross-team coordination, a staff should show organizational leadership and vision-setting
- Portfolio as a UX artifact in itself — 94% of first impressions are design-related, meaning your portfolio must demonstrate good UX . Readability, scannability, and information hierarchy matter enormously.
- Curated intentionality over comprehensive documentation — the shift in 2026 is from 'I did all these things' to 'Here's what I chose to show and why it matters for you'
- Adaptability signals — evidence of working with AI, new tools, or emerging design challenges shows you're future-ready
